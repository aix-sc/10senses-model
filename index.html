01 — The Thesis

When an AI claims correctness, correct for whom?

For decades, AI and information-retrieval evaluation has treated correctness as fidelity to ground truth — Precision, Recall, BERTScore, ROUGE. These measures excel where a single right answer can be defined. But they are constitutively unable to ask the questions that matter once AI begins to guide human decisions: right for whom, in what context, at what moment.

Scientific accuracy is a necessary condition for a reliable AI output. It is not a sufficient one. A diagnosis that ignores a patient's life circumstances, an investment that ignores a client's risk tolerance, a training plan that ignores how an athlete actually feels — each can be measurably correct and practically wrong at the same time.

This work formalizes that gap. It treats contextual appropriateness not as a soft afterthought but as a first-class, computable property of output quality.

17.1%
In a large-scale validation across human–AI coaching sessions, subjective and objective intensity diverged in roughly one session in six — a structural blind spot that accuracy-only systems cannot, by construction, surface. This not only raises injury risk, but also erodes the trust between athlete and coach.
02 — Why It Matters

If data science exists to give meaning to numbers drawn from human and social reality, then evaluating its outputs cannot stop at scientific Truth — the criterion of pure science. The measures that matter must be human and social as well. That is the gap this framework is built to close.

Why data science is a science

Computer science gives form to the nature of computation the brain itself holds. Data science is something else: it imagines real-world phenomena — social, natural, and human — and materializes them by computing them inside the machine. Above all, it learns to give names to data. It is, in short, the science that gives meaning to numbers.

— Yasushi Kiyoki, Dean, Faculty of Data Science (MIDS), Musashino University
03 — Epistemological Foundation

An ancient triad, extended for AI

The framework rests on the three modes of recognition that have survived two and a half millennia of philosophical scrutiny since Plato, systematized in Kant's Critique of Judgment: Truth (aletheia), Good (agathon), and Beauty (kalon). To these, the model adds two dimensions essential once AI meets a human: Relation and a Moderator.

07 — The Moderator

Making "something feels off" computable

M1 is the one term in the TCI that can only subtract — never inflate the score. It is also the framework's most distinctive move: it treats human intuitive dissonance as signal, not noise. When the numbers look fine but perception disagrees, M1 holds the confidence score back and opens a conversation — rather than confidently confirming an answer that may be wrong.

11 — In Plain Language

Frequently Asked Questions

No jargon. If you're new to all of this, start here.

Q1What is this research actually about?
When an AI gives you an answer, it's usually judged on one thing: is it factually correct? This research argues that's not enough. An answer can be perfectly correct and still be wrong for you, right now. The 10 Senses Model adds four more things to weigh beyond the science — whether it fits your goals, whether it's simple and clear, whether you trust the source, and whether something just feels off — and folds them into a single trust score. In the real running data behind this work, what people felt and what the numbers said disagreed in 17.1% of sessions — about one in six — which is exactly the blind spot a correctness-only system can't see.
Q2What does "Truth is not enough" mean?
Say a coach tells you "run 10 km today." That can be scientifically correct training — and still a bad idea if you're exhausted, carrying an injury, or you'd dread every step. Truth (the science) is necessary, but so is whether it's good for you (your goals and preferences), whether it's simple and harmonious enough to actually stick to, whether you trust the source, and whether your gut says something's wrong. The framework weighs all five families at once, so a recommendation that is "correct but cold," "correct but too complex," or "correct but badly timed" no longer slips through unnoticed.
Q3What are the "10 senses"?
They're ten ways of judging whether an AI's answer can be trusted, grouped into five families. Truth (4 senses): the science, how you compare to similar people, what an expert would say, and your own training history. Good (2): your goals, and your preferences and satisfaction. Beauty (1): how simple and harmonious the plan is. Relation (2): how much you trust the coach, and how warm it feels. The tenth, the Moderator (M1), is special — a gut-feeling alarm that doesn't add points but can hold the whole score back when something doesn't add up.
Q4What is the TCI score?
The Training Confidence Index folds the senses into a single score from 0 to 100, shown as a traffic light: green (≥ 75) means go, yellow (50–74) means look closer, red (≤ 49) means caution. It's built in layers — a scientific foundation, then execution, quality, and relationship, combined with set weights — and every sense is measured twice: once from the device data and once from what you actually reported feeling, then reconciled into one number.
Q5What is the "M1 intuitive alert"?
It's that gut-feeling alarm. Sometimes every number looks fine, but something is off — your watch logs an "easy" Zone-1 effort while you rate it brutal. M1 catches the mismatch and, instead of cheerfully saying "great job," it holds the confidence score back and asks a clarifying question. In the actual data, one identical 23.6 km easy walk drew warm praise at RPE 2 but a gentle "what made this feel hard?" at RPE 8 — same objective numbers, different felt experience, deliberately different response.
Q6Why test it on runners?
Running produces honest, measurable data — distance, pace, heart rate — paired with how hard it actually felt, which makes the gap between the two visible. This work draws on 299 runners and 20,343 sessions over five months. And for elite athletes at Olympic-qualifying level (VDOT 70–76), one mistimed call can forfeit a competitive window that comes only once every four years — so getting the judgment right is anything but academic. The same five-category logic transfers directly to clinical decisions, financial advice, and legal reasoning.
Q7Is anyone's personal data shown here?
No. Everything on this page is either a big-picture total (like "299 runners" or "160,876 km") or an example already published in the paper with all identifying details removed. The downloadable dataset contains only aggregate counts — no individual records, names, locations, or free text. No individual's private data is shown or shared here.
Anticipated questions
Q8Can 10SM be used in other domains?
Yes — and that is really the whole point. Think of the framework as five empty drawers labelled Truth, Good, Beauty, Relation, and a safety check we call the Moderator. The drawers themselves don't care what subject you work on; they are general. What changes from field to field is what you put inside them. In this paper we filled the drawers for running, and the result is the TCI score. To use the same idea in medicine, money, or school, you would refill the drawers with the measurements that matter there. Doing exactly that is one of our main next steps, and we think it helps most where an AI is helping make a serious decision — your health, your money, your future — places where being merely "correct" is not good enough.
Q9Doesn't the M1 Intuitive Alert cause false positives?
It is built specifically to avoid that. M1 stays completely silent unless your own feeling and the cold numbers disagree by a lot — say the data shows an easy stroll, but you rated it brutally hard. Even when it does notice, it does not sound an alarm or overrule you. It simply asks a gentle question: "That looked easy on paper — what made it feel tough today?" Maybe you slept badly, maybe you are coming down with something, maybe it was hot. A system that shouts false alarms is annoying, and people quickly stop trusting it. A system that only asks a question costs you almost nothing — you answer in a second, or ignore it. By choosing a question over a warning, M1 keeps the cost of a wrong guess very small.
Q10How is this different from G-Eval or RAGAS?
G-Eval and RAGAS are two popular tools for grading an AI's answers. They mostly check one thing: does the answer match a trusted reference — is it consistent, faithful to its source, and on-topic? That matters, but in our terms it all lives inside just one of the five families: Truth. The 10 Senses Model does not throw those tools away — it surrounds them. On top of Truth it also weighs whether the answer is good for you, simple and clear, from a source you trust, and whether anything feels off. So we do not compete with G-Eval or RAGAS; we give them a bigger frame to sit in. You can take a G-Eval score, drop it into the Truth drawer of our model, and then ask the four questions it was never designed to ask. Picture G-Eval as an excellent thermometer, and 10SM as the full check-up that the thermometer is just one part of.
Q11Can objectivity hold when the data is all your own?
This is a fair worry, and we take it seriously. The data behind this study comes from EKIDEN.AI, a coaching platform that our own organization runs and funds. When the people testing an idea also own the thing being tested, there is a real risk of seeing what you want to see — that is called a conflict of interest. We do not hide this. We state it plainly in the paper and try to cancel it out by working in the open: exactly how the data was collected, exactly what we did with it, and exactly what came out, so anyone can follow the steps and push back. The strongest test is for an outside group with no stake in the result to repeat the study on their own data and see whether they reach the same answer. We actively encourage that, and it is a direction we want to pursue.
Q12Why call them "Senses"?
The name comes from an old idea by the thinker Rudolf Steiner, who argued that people really have twelve senses, not just the five — sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell — you learn in school, including subtler ones like a sense of balance or a feel for another person's mood. We do not copy his system point for point. What we borrow is his core insight: that we take in the world through many channels at once, not one. In this model a "Sense" has nothing to do with eyes or ears — it is a way of judging an AI's answer. Each Sense is like a different question your mind asks: "Is it true? Is it good for me? Is it clear? Do I trust it? Does something feel off?" Calling them Senses is a reminder that judging an answer well, like understanding the world, takes more than a single channel.
The math, step by step
Q13Equation 1: how is each individual Sense scored?
Equation 1 is Sᵢ = αᵢ·Oᵢ + (1 − αᵢ)·Uᵢ, and it is the very first step: it produces one 0–10 score for a single Sense by blending two views of the same thing. Oᵢ is the objective number the system measures — what the data says (for example, the pace and heart-rate the device recorded). Uᵢ is the subjective number the person reports — what it actually felt like (for example, "that was an 8-out-of-10 effort"). Sᵢ is the resulting combined score for Sense i.

The dial in the middle is αᵢ (alpha), a number between 0 and 1 that decides how much to lean on the data versus the feeling, set separately for each Sense. Notice the two weights always add up to 1: if the objective side gets αᵢ, the subjective side automatically gets the rest, (1 − αᵢ). That is on purpose — because the weights sum to one, the blended score stays on the same clean 0–10 scale as its two ingredients instead of drifting larger or smaller.

Why let alpha change from Sense to Sense? Because some Senses are mostly a matter of hard data and some are mostly a matter of how a person feels. For a Sense like T1 Scientific — built from device measurements such as training load and heart-rate — alpha is high, so the data leads. For a Sense like G2 Preference or R2 Relational Warmth — which are about enjoyment, trust, and feeling connected — alpha is low, so the person's own report leads. The equation is simply a fair way to mix "what the machine sees" and "what the human knows" in the right proportion for each Sense, instead of pretending either one is the whole story.
Q14Equation 2: how do Sense scores combine into a layer?
Equation 2 is Lₖ = Σ wᵢ·Sᵢ. Equation 1 gave a 0–10 score for each individual Sense; Equation 2 gathers the Senses that belong together into one "layer" score. The big Σ (sigma) just means "add these up," and the sum runs over every Sense i that belongs to layer k. So Lₖ — the score for layer k — is the weighted sum of the Sense scores inside it.

Each Sense gets its own importance weight, wᵢ, and within a layer these weights are arranged to add up to 1, so the layer score again lands on the familiar 0–10 scale. A Sense judged more important to that layer simply carries a larger wᵢ and pulls the layer score toward itself more strongly.

The Senses are grouped this way because they naturally do different jobs. The Foundation layer holds the bedrock Senses — scientific evidence (T1) and the person's own training history (T4). The Execution layer holds the Senses about whether a plan is actually doable and fits the individual — population norms (T2), expert guidance (T3), and the person's goals and preferences (G1, G2). The Quality layer holds the Senses about the human interface — simplicity and harmony (B1) and the trust and warmth of the relationship (R1, R2). Rolling related Senses into one layer score keeps the final calculation tidy and lets each layer be weighted as a whole in the next step.
Q15Equation 3: how is the final TCI produced — and how can M1 hold it back?
Equation 3 is TCI = f(M₁) · Σ βₖ·Lₖ → [0, 100], and it is where everything comes together into a single confidence score. First, the layer scores from Equation 2 are combined using their own weights, βₖ (beta) — one per layer. The paper's default betas are Foundation 0.30, Execution 0.35, Quality 0.25, and Relation 0.10; they add up to 1.00, so the weighted sum stays on a 0–10 scale before being stretched to the final 0–100 range.

Those numbers encode a deliberate order of priority. Execution carries the most weight (0.35) because that is where most actionable coaching lives — whether a plan is feasible and fits this person is what most directly shapes a good recommendation. Foundation is just behind (0.30): the science and the athlete's own history are the bedrock everything else rests on. Quality (0.25) — simplicity and harmony — decides whether a plan is actually followed, but sits below the first two. Relation (0.10) is smallest, partly because trust and warmth are the softest, most subjective signals and the hardest to measure reliably today. Importantly, these are empirically chosen starting values, not fixed constants: the paper states plainly that they can be tuned for a different use case, and that learning them automatically from data is planned future work.

The most distinctive part is f(M₁) sitting out front. M1, the Intuitive Alert, measures the absence of an "off" feeling, and f(M₁) turns it into a multiplier between 0 and 1. When perception and data agree, f(M₁) sits at 1 and changes nothing. But when there is a big mismatch — the data says "easy," the athlete says "brutal" — f(M₁) drops below 1 and scales the whole score down. Because M1 enters by multiplying, never adding, it can only ever pull the confidence score back; it can never inflate it. That is what lets a single strong "something feels off" signal override an otherwise high score, exactly as an experienced coach would. Finally the result is mapped onto 0–100 and read as a zone: GREEN (75 and above, high confidence), YELLOW (50–74, moderate), or RED (49 and below, needs attention).

How 10SM relates to — and differs from — the prior work it builds on, reference by reference.

Related work · AI output evaluation
[3]G-Eval — Liu et al. (2023)
G-Eval uses a powerful AI (GPT-4) as an automatic grader: it reads a piece of text, thinks step by step, and gives it a score — agreeing with human graders about half the time (a 0.51 correlation) on summary tasks. Like other "let an AI grade the AI" methods, it mostly checks one thing: how closely the answer matches a correct reference. That is useful, but in our terms it is only the Truth category. 10SM keeps that accuracy check and then adds four more questions on top: does it fit you, do you trust it, is it clear and simple, and does anything feel off?
[4]RAGAS — Es et al. (2023)
"RAG" means an AI that first looks things up in a document store and then answers using what it found. RAGAS grades that kind of system on three points: did it stay faithful to the sources, was the answer on-topic, and did it pull up the right material? It makes accuracy-checking for these systems much sharper — but, just like G-Eval, it never asks whether the answer is the right one for this person, in this context. That blind spot is exactly the gap 10SM is built to close.
Related work · Training-load science
[5]Fitness–Fatigue Model — Banister (1991)
This is a classic sports-science idea: every workout builds up two things at once — fitness (good) and fatigue (tiring) — and your real performance on any day is the tug-of-war between them. It is solid science, and 10SM feeds models like this straight into its Truth dimension (T1 Scientific) as objective input. But the fitness-minus-fatigue number is only the foundation: it still doesn't tell you whether today's plan fits the person, so it isn't the whole judgment.
[6]Training Stress Score (TSS) — Coggan (2003)
TSS (Training Stress Score) squeezes a whole workout — how hard and how long — into one tidy number, so you can compare different days at a glance. In 10SM, that number is one of the objective signals feeding the Truth dimension (T1 Scientific). It is a handy summary and we use it — but a single number cannot see how the athlete actually felt during the session, which is exactly why we pair it with subjective signals.
[7]Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio — Hulin et al. (2016)
This study found a simple warning sign for injuries: compare how much you trained in the last week against your usual amount over the last month. If the recent load jumps far above your normal, your injury risk climbs. 10SM borrows their "safe zone" for that ratio (0.8 to 1.3) and builds it into the Truth dimension (T1 Scientific) — then adds checks for how you feel and how you and your coach are communicating on top of it.
[8]Session-RPE — Foster et al. (2001)
This paper introduced a clever, low-tech way to measure how hard a workout was: just multiply how hard it felt (on a simple scale) by how long it lasted — no expensive gadgets needed. 10SM uses that "how hard it felt" rating as a core subjective signal, but instead of trusting it alone, it lines it up against the objective intensity from the data. When the two disagree (the 17.1% finding), that mismatch becomes visible.
[9]Hooper Index — Hooper et al. (1995)
The Hooper Index watches for overtraining by asking the athlete four everyday questions — how well did you sleep, how stressed are you, how tired, how sore? It captures the human, felt side that pure number-crunching misses. 10SM takes that idea further by organizing both layers — the felt, subjective side and the measured, objective side — under one combined score, so neither one gets ignored.
[22]RPE scale — Borg (1982)
Borg created the original scale that turns a feeling — "how hard is this effort?" — into a number you can write down and track. It is the foundation of nearly all perceived-effort measurement. In 10SM, that "how hard it feels" number is the subjective-intensity input, and the whole system works by comparing it against the objective intensity from the data to spot where the two disagree.
[23]VDOT — Daniels (2014)
Coach Jack Daniels' famous "Running Formula" boils a runner's current ability down to one number, VDOT, calculated from a recent race result — and from it you can read off the right pace for each kind of training. 10SM uses VDOT-based intensity as its objective yardstick: the firm, data-side reference that the runner's subjective "how hard it felt" rating is checked against.
[19]AI for injury prediction — Claudino et al. (2019)
This is a big survey paper that gathered up many studies using AI to predict injuries and performance in team sports. Those systems are good at one job: forecasting what might happen. But they stop there — they do not judge how trustworthy a piece of advice is, or explain why. 10SM is aimed at the opposite job: not predicting an outcome, but evaluating how reliable a recommendation is and showing the reasoning behind it, so a person can decide whether to follow it.
Related work · Epistemology & knowledge bases
[1]The twelve senses — Steiner (1916)
Over a century ago, the thinker Rudolf Steiner argued that people actually perceive the world through twelve senses, not just the familiar five — adding subtler ones like balance, warmth, and even a sense for another person. 10SM borrows the spirit of that idea — that perception has many dimensions, not one — without copying his specific system. Two of our senses, R1 Coach Trust and R2 Relational Warmth, deliberately echo his "ego" sense (feeling another person) and his "warmth" sense.
[10]Beauty and Revolution in Science — McAllister (1996)
This book showed, with real historical evidence, that scientists do not pick theories on cold facts alone — they are also drawn to ones that are elegant, simple, and harmonious. In other words, a sense of beauty quietly guides serious science. That backs up a key 10SM claim: Beauty — simplicity and harmony in an answer — is a genuine way to judge quality, not just decoration added on top.
[11]The Structure of Scientific Revolutions — Kuhn (1962)
Kuhn's hugely influential book argued that when science shifts from one big worldview ("paradigm") to another, the choice is not made by evidence alone — a sense of which theory is more elegant plays a real part. Together with McAllister, this gives 10SM solid grounds for treating B1 Aesthetic Harmony as a serious dimension to evaluate, not a soft extra.
[2]The physicist's picture of nature — Dirac (1963)
Paul Dirac, one of the great physicists, famously held that a physical theory ought to be mathematically beautiful — he trusted elegance so much that he treated it as a sign of being on the right track. His view is one of the foundations 10SM points to when it argues that Beauty can be a real marker of a good answer, not just a matter of taste.
[20]Truth and Method — Gadamer (1960)
Gadamer was a philosopher who studied how we understand things ("hermeneutics"). His core point: understanding is never neutral — it is always shaped by your situation, your background, and the moment you are in. That directly supports a starting premise of 10SM: what counts as the "correct" answer depends on the context and the person, which is why the same fact can be right for one runner and wrong for another.
[12]Federated database systems — Sheth & Larson (1990)
This landmark paper laid out how to make many separate, differently-built databases work together as one, while each keeps its own format and independence. It is a computer-systems ancestor of a core 10SM idea: pulling together many differently-shaped sources of information — numbers, feelings, relationships — and evaluating across all of them at once instead of one at a time.
[13][14]Semantic interoperability — Kiyoki et al. (1995, 2002)
This line of work built a mathematical way to capture meaning, so that databases that describe the world differently can still understand each other (called "semantic interoperability"). For 10SM it provides the conceptual toolkit for fusing signals of completely different shapes — hard numbers, personal feelings, relationship cues — into a single, coherent assessment instead of leaving them in separate silos.
[15]Meta-Chronicle — Ishibashi & Kiyoki (2004)
Meta-Chronicle was a system for handling data spread across both space and time, drawing on many databases at once. It is a direct ancestor in 10SM's family tree: the same basic instinct of bringing together many sources and several layers, and reasoning over all of them together rather than in isolation.
[16]Career-opportunity knowledge base — Takahashi & Kiyoki (2008)
This earlier system helped people discover career opportunities by linking together very different databases about jobs and education and reasoning across them. Of all the prior work, it is the closest cousin to 10SM: the same pattern of layered evaluation across many unlike sources — applied to careers then, and to judging AI advice now.
Related work · Validation statistics
[17]Cohen's Kappa — Cohen (1960)
When two people score the same things, some of their agreement could just be luck. Cohen's Kappa is a number that measures how much they truly agree after subtracting that lucky-guess part (a value of 0.61 or higher counts as "substantial" agreement). 10SM uses it to check that its expert raters are genuinely consistent with each other, rather than agreeing by accident.
[18]Cronbach's alpha — Cronbach (1951)
If a questionnaire asks several questions that are all meant to measure the same thing, Cronbach's alpha checks whether they actually hang together — whether the items tell a consistent story. 10SM uses it, together with two common correlation measures (Spearman and Pearson), to confirm that its expert ratings are internally consistent and therefore trustworthy.
Related work · Measurement blueprint
[24]Seven dimensions of human action — Iwata (2024)
Iwata proposes a way to describe human experience across seven dimensions at once, rather than flattening it into a single measure. 10SM adopts this as the blueprint for how it will test itself in the future: measuring the TCI's real effects on the body (the Physio dimension, e.g. heart-rate variability), on the mind (the Psycho dimension, e.g. perceived effort and satisfaction), and on relationships (the Social dimension, e.g. the conversation between coach and athlete).

01 — The Thesis

When an AI claims correctness, correct for whom?

For decades, AI and information-retrieval evaluation has treated correctness as fidelity to ground truth — Precision, Recall, BERTScore, ROUGE. These measures excel where a single right answer can be defined. But they are constitutively unable to ask the questions that matter once AI begins to guide human decisions: right for whom, in what context, at what moment.

Scientific accuracy is a necessary condition for a reliable AI output. It is not a sufficient one. A diagnosis that ignores a patient's life circumstances, an investment that ignores a client's risk tolerance, a training plan that ignores how an athlete actually feels — each can be measurably correct and practically wrong at the same time.

This work formalizes that gap. It treats contextual appropriateness not as a soft afterthought but as a first-class, computable property of output quality.

02 — Epistemological Foundation

An ancient triad, extended for AI

The framework rests on the three modes of recognition that have survived two and a half millennia of philosophical scrutiny since Plato, systematized in Kant's Critique of Judgment: Truth (aletheia), Good (agathon), and Beauty (kalon). To these, the model adds two dimensions essential once AI meets a human: Relation and a Moderator.

Ten cognitive senses for judging the reliability of an AI output — mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive within the space of value judgment. Nine contribute to the score; one watches over all of them.

05 — Operationalization

The Training Confidence Index

The 10 Senses become computable through the TCI — a hierarchical index that fuses what the system measures with what the person reports, then lets intuition override the arithmetic when it must.

06 — The Moderator

Making "something feels off" computable

M1 is the framework's most distinctive move: it treats human intuitive dissonance as signal, not noise. When the numbers look fine but perception disagrees, M1 holds the confidence score back and opens a conversation — rather than confidently confirming an answer that may be wrong.

Accuracy-only system

An easy-intensity session is logged — but the person reports it felt extremely hard.

"Your metrics look great. Freshness is high — you're well prepared. Keep it up."

→ confirms · contradiction unseen
10SM · M1 triggered

The same session — objective load and perceived effort sharply disagree.

"A very hard effort on an easy session is unusual. What made it feel that way — fatigue, terrain, something else? Let's clarify before deciding."

→ score suppressed · dialogue opened

This is the difference between an evaluation that asks only what an output measures and one that also asks what it might be missing. The example is illustrative; the mechanism is the contribution.

07 — The Dataset

A portrait of the data

Five months of real coaching on EKIDEN.AI, from first-time joggers to Olympic-level runners. Everything below is aggregate and non-identifying — and openly published.

What EKIDEN.AI is

The data comes from EKIDEN.AI — an agentic AI running coach delivered largely through email. It computes the same indicators elite coaches rely on, weighs every session against the runner's own goals, and — crucially — folds in subjective effort and free-text feedback alongside the device data. Its founding ambition is the first professional running team coached entirely by AI.

Why this data was used
01

It fuses objective signals — Garmin device and physiological data, weather context — with the runner's own subjective report. That is precisely where measurable accuracy and felt experience can diverge, which is the gap this framework measures.

02

Its runners span first-timers to national-record holders and Olympians, so the cost of a "correct but wrong" recommendation ranges from trivial to career-defining — the full stakes spectrum in one dataset.

03

EKIDEN.AI is built on a human–AI hybrid principle: don't trust AI alone, don't read only the numbers, understand the runner's story. That makes it a natural testbed for a framework arguing that truth is necessary but not sufficient.

10 — In Plain Language

Frequently Asked Questions

No jargon. If you're new to all of this, start here.

Q1What is this research actually about?
When an AI gives you an answer, it's usually judged on one thing: is it factually correct? This research argues that's not enough. An answer can be perfectly correct and still be wrong for you, right now. The 10 Senses Model adds four more things to weigh beyond the science — whether it fits your goals, whether it's simple and clear, whether you trust the source, and whether something just feels off — and folds them into a single trust score. In the real running data behind this work, what people felt and what the numbers said disagreed in 17.1% of sessions — about one in six — which is exactly the blind spot a correctness-only system can't see.
Q2What does "Truth is not enough" mean?
Say a coach tells you "run 10 km today." That can be scientifically correct training — and still a bad idea if you're exhausted, carrying an injury, or you'd dread every step. Truth (the science) is necessary, but so is whether it's good for you (your goals and preferences), whether it's simple and harmonious enough to actually stick to, whether you trust the source, and whether your gut says something's wrong. The framework weighs all five families at once, so a recommendation that is "correct but cold," "correct but too complex," or "correct but badly timed" no longer slips through unnoticed.
Q3What are the "10 senses"?
They're ten ways of judging whether an AI's answer can be trusted, grouped into five families. Truth (4 senses): the science, how you compare to similar people, what an expert would say, and your own training history. Good (2): your goals, and your preferences and satisfaction. Beauty (1): how simple and harmonious the plan is. Relation (2): how much you trust the coach, and how warm it feels. The tenth, the Moderator (M1), is special — a gut-feeling alarm that doesn't add points but can hold the whole score back when something doesn't add up.
Q4What is the TCI score?
The Training Confidence Index folds the senses into a single score from 0 to 100, shown as a traffic light: green (≥ 75) means go, yellow (50–74) means look closer, red (≤ 49) means caution. It's built in layers — a scientific foundation, then execution, quality, and relationship, combined with set weights — and every sense is measured twice: once from the device data and once from what you actually reported feeling, then reconciled into one number.
Q5What is the "M1 intuitive alert"?
It's that gut-feeling alarm. Sometimes every number looks fine, but something is off — your watch logs an "easy" Zone-1 effort while you rate it brutal. M1 catches the mismatch and, instead of cheerfully saying "great job," it holds the confidence score back and asks a clarifying question. In the actual data, one identical 23.6 km easy walk drew warm praise at RPE 2 but a gentle "what made this feel hard?" at RPE 8 — same objective numbers, different felt experience, deliberately different response.
Q6Why test it on runners?
Running produces honest, measurable data — distance, pace, heart rate — paired with how hard it actually felt, which makes the gap between the two visible. This work draws on 299 runners and 20,343 sessions over five months. And for elite athletes at Olympic-qualifying level (VDOT 70–76), one mistimed call can forfeit a competitive window that comes only once every four years — so getting the judgment right is anything but academic. The same five-category logic transfers directly to clinical decisions, financial advice, and legal reasoning.
Q7Is anyone's personal data shown here?
No. Everything on this page is either a big-picture total (like "299 runners" or "160,876 km") or an example already published in the paper with all identifying details removed. The downloadable dataset contains only aggregate counts — no individual records, names, locations, or free text. No individual's private data is shown or shared here.

How 10SM relates to — and differs from — the prior work it builds on, reference by reference.

Related work · AI output evaluation
[3]G-Eval — Liu et al. (2023)
Uses GPT-4 with chain-of-thought reasoning as an automatic judge of generated text, reaching about 0.51 correlation with human ratings on summarization. Like other "LLM-as-judge" methods it still measures fidelity to a ground truth; 10SM keeps that accuracy as one category (Truth) and adds individual fit, trust, harmony, and an intuitive-dissonance check.
[4]RAGAS — Es et al. (2023)
Scores retrieval-augmented systems on faithfulness, answer relevancy, and context precision. It sharpens accuracy measurement for RAG, but — like G-Eval — never asks whether an answer is right for this person, in this context. That is exactly the gap 10SM is built to close.
Related work · Training-load science
[5]Fitness–Fatigue Model — Banister (1991)
Models performance as the running balance between accumulated fitness and fatigue from training. 10SM treats such physiological models as inputs to the Truth dimension (T1 Scientific) — foundational, but not the whole judgment.
[6]Training Stress Score (TSS) — Coggan (2003)
Compresses the load of a session into a single number. In 10SM, TSS is one objective signal feeding T1 Scientific — necessary, but blind to how the athlete actually felt.
[7]Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio — Hulin et al. (2016)
Showed that the ratio of recent to longer-term training load predicts injury risk. 10SM adopts the ACWR safe range (0.8–1.3) inside T1 Scientific, then layers subjective and relational checks on top of it.
[8]Session-RPE — Foster et al. (2001)
Introduced session-RPE — perceived exertion times duration — as a simple subjective load measure. 10SM uses RPE as a core subjective signal, but pairs it with objective intensity so mismatches (the 17.1% finding) become visible.
[9]Hooper Index — Hooper et al. (1995)
Monitors overtraining via sleep, stress, fatigue, and muscle soreness. It addresses the subjective side that accuracy metrics miss; 10SM systematizes both the subjective and objective layers under a single index.
[22]RPE scale — Borg (1982)
The psychophysical scale that turns "how hard it feels" into a number. It underpins the subjective-intensity input that 10SM compares against objective intensity to detect discrepancy.
[23]VDOT — Daniels (2014)
Daniels' Running Formula defines VDOT, a single scale of fitness and intensity derived from race performance. 10SM uses VDOT-based intensity as the objective reference that subjective RPE is checked against.
[19]AI for injury prediction — Claudino et al. (2019)
A systematic review of AI for injury-risk and performance prediction in team sports. Such systems predict outcomes, but do not evaluate or explain the reliability of a recommendation; 10SM focuses on evaluating that reliability and surfacing the reasoning behind it.
Related work · Epistemology & knowledge bases
[1]The twelve senses — Steiner (1916)
Steiner proposed that humans perceive through twelve senses, not five. 10SM borrows the spirit of that idea — perception is multi-dimensional — without adopting his system; two senses (R1 Coach Trust, R2 Relational Warmth) echo his "ego" and "warmth" senses.
[10]Beauty and Revolution in Science — McAllister (1996)
Showed empirically that scientists use aesthetic criteria when choosing between theories. This grounds 10SM's claim that Beauty — simplicity and harmony — is a legitimate evaluation axis, not decoration.
[11]The Structure of Scientific Revolutions — Kuhn (1962)
Argued that aesthetic judgement plays a real role in how scientists choose paradigms. Together with McAllister, it justifies including B1 Aesthetic Harmony as a serious evaluation dimension.
[2]The physicist's picture of nature — Dirac (1963)
Dirac argued that a physical theory should possess mathematical beauty. It sits among the foundations 10SM cites for treating Beauty as a genuine marker of a good output.
[20]Truth and Method — Gadamer (1960)
Gadamer's hermeneutics holds that understanding is always shaped by context and the interpreter's situation. This grounds 10SM's premise that what counts as "correct" depends on context, and informs its sense-as-evaluation framing.
[12]Federated database systems — Sheth & Larson (1990)
Defined how to integrate heterogeneous, autonomous databases. It is a systems-side precursor to 10SM's idea of evaluating across many differently-shaped data sources at once.
[13][14]Semantic interoperability — Kiyoki et al. (1995, 2002)
A mathematical model of meaning enabling semantic interoperability across databases. It provides the conceptual basis for fusing differently-shaped signals — objective, subjective, relational — into one assessment.
[15]Meta-Chronicle — Ishibashi & Kiyoki (2004)
A spatial-temporal multidatabase system. A direct lineage ancestor of 10SM's multi-source, multi-layer evaluation approach.
[16]Career-opportunity knowledge base — Takahashi & Kiyoki (2008)
A meta-level knowledge-base system that discovers career opportunities by connecting heterogeneous occupational and educational databases. It is the closest conceptual precursor to 10SM: multi-layer evaluation across heterogeneous sources.
Related work · Validation statistics
[17]Cohen's Kappa — Cohen (1960)
A measure of agreement between raters that corrects for chance (κ ≥ 0.61 is "substantial"). 10SM uses it to check the inter-rater reliability of its expert evaluations.
[18]Cronbach's alpha — Cronbach (1951)
Measures the internal consistency of a multi-item scale. 10SM uses it, alongside Spearman and Pearson correlations, to validate the consistency of expert ratings.
Related work · Measurement blueprint
[24]Seven dimensions of human action — Iwata (2024)
Proposes a seven-dimensional model for describing human experience. 10SM adopts it as the blueprint for future validation — measuring TCI's effects across the Physio dimension (HRV), the Psycho dimension (RPE and satisfaction), and the Social dimension (coach–athlete dialogue).
